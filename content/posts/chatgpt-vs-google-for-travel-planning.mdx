The great travel planning debate isn't between guidebooks and blogs anymore—it's between ChatGPT and Google. After planning dozens of trips using both, here's the truth: ChatGPT excels at creative brainstorming but fails at execution, while Google drowns you in options without context. ChatGPT can't book flights or verify current prices, and Google's travel tools prioritize whoever pays the most for ad placement.

The real winner? Purpose-built travel AI that combines ChatGPT's conversational ease with Google's real-time data—and actually handles the booking.

---

## Where ChatGPT shines (and where it doesn't)

ChatGPT is genuinely brilliant for the early stages of trip planning. Ask it to design a 10-day Japan itinerary mixing cities and nature, and you'll get thoughtful suggestions that consider travel time, seasonal factors, and logical routing. It's particularly good at understanding nuanced requests like "romantic but not touristy" or "family-friendly with teenage kids who hate museums."

I tested this extensively for a recent client trip to Morocco. ChatGPT suggested a perfect progression from Casablanca to Fez to the Atlas Mountains to Marrakech, with specific recommendations for Riad Fes Relais & Châteaux in the medina and La Mamounia in Marrakech, plus realistic 4-hour travel times between cities.

> "ChatGPT understands context and nuance in ways that traditional search simply can't match—but that brilliance stops the moment you need real prices or actual bookings."

But here's where ChatGPT hits a wall: it can't access real-time flight prices, hotel availability, or current schedules. When I asked it to find flights from LAX to Casablanca in March, it gave me helpful general advice about connecting through Europe but couldn't tell me that Royal Air Maroc's direct flight was $1,247 compared to the $1,647 Air France connection it suggested.

Worse, ChatGPT's training data has a cutoff date, so it doesn't know about new routes, airline changes, or current travel restrictions. It once confidently recommended Hotel Empress Zoe in Istanbul that had been closed for renovations for eight months.

---

## Google's travel tools: powerful but overwhelming

Google Travel represents the opposite approach—maximum information with minimal curation. Google Flights shows you every possible routing with precise prices updated in real-time. Google Hotels displays hundreds of properties with detailed filters and guest photos.

The data accuracy is unmatched. When Google Flights shows a $847 United flight from JFK to Barcelona, that price is current and bookable. The calendar view revealing that flying Tuesday instead of Friday saves $186 is genuinely useful. And the price tracking alerts have saved me thousands over the years.

But Google's comprehensiveness becomes its weakness. Search for hotels in Rome's Trastevere neighborhood and you'll face 1,200+ options ranging from €25 hostels near Termini Station to €2,000 suites at Hotel de Russie. The ranking algorithm prioritizes whoever pays for better placement, not necessarily what's best for your trip.

> "Google gives you all the data but none of the wisdom—it's like having access to every restaurant in a city but no friend to tell you which ones are actually worth visiting."

Google also struggles with complex, multi-city itineraries. Try planning a trip that combines flights from Boston to Amsterdam, trains from Amsterdam to Prague via Berlin, and hotels across multiple countries, and you'll end up with 17 browser tabs and a spreadsheet that would make a project manager weep.

## The real-world test: planning a complex trip

I recently compared both tools while planning a three-week European trip hitting Amsterdam, Prague, Vienna, and Barcelona with a $4,500 budget. Here's how they performed:

**ChatGPT's approach:**
- Suggested logical city progression: Amsterdam → Prague → Vienna → Barcelona based on geography and cultural flow
- Recommended specific neighborhoods like Jordaan in Amsterdam and Vinohrady in Prague with reasoning
- Provided detailed daily itineraries with realistic 3-4 hour sightseeing blocks
- Failed completely at finding actual flights and hotel prices
- Couldn't handle the complexity of open-jaw flights (Boston-Amsterdam, Barcelona-Boston) and city-to-city trains

**Google's approach:**
- Found exact flight prices: $542 Delta to Amsterdam, $398 Vueling return from Barcelona
- Showed thousands of hotel options with current availability from €45-€400 per night
- Provided accurate train schedules: Amsterdam-Prague 14h 32m via DB, €79
- Overwhelmed me with choices lacking any editorial perspective
- Required constant cross-referencing between Google Flights, Google Hotels, and separate train booking sites

Neither approach alone created a bookable trip. ChatGPT gave me the framework but no execution path. Google gave me the raw materials but no blueprint for assembly.

Until every travel service evolves to match this integrated approach, here's the most effective strategy I've developed:

**Start with ChatGPT for:**
- Initial destination brainstorming ("3 weeks in Europe, mix of art cities and coastal towns")
- Rough itinerary structure and logical city progression
- Understanding cultural context and optimal timing
- Getting ideas for unique experiences beyond standard tourist attractions

**Move to specialized tools like Otherwhere for:**
- Actual flight and hotel searches with current prices
- Real availability checking for specific dates
- Complex routing with multiple cities and mixed transportation
- Final booking and confirmation with 24/7 support

**Use Google as backup for:**
- Verifying prices on specific routes if something seems off
- Finding alternative options when first choices are unavailable
- Reading recent reviews and checking updated hotel photos

The key is recognizing each tool's strengths and not expecting any single platform to excel at everything. ChatGPT for creativity, specialized travel AI for execution, and Google for verification.
