The corner office executive who bills $300 per hour shouldn't spend three hours comparing flight prices to save $50. Yet that's exactly what most high-earning professionals do when planning personal travel. A growing number are doing the math differently—outsourcing solo travel planning to personal concierges who handle everything from research to booking.

The shift represents a fundamental change in how successful professionals think about time. When your hourly rate exceeds what most people make in a day, the old rules of DIY travel planning stop making financial sense.

---

## The hidden cost of DIY travel planning

Most professionals vastly underestimate the time they spend planning trips. A recent study by the Global Business Travel Association found that travelers spend an average of 4.2 hours researching and booking a single leisure trip.

For complex itineraries—say, a two-week solo adventure through Japan with specific hotel preferences in Kyoto's Gion district and Tokyo's Shibuya, plus JR Pass coordination and loyalty program considerations—that number easily doubles. You're looking at 8-10 hours of research, comparison shopping, and coordination.

Sarah Chen, a partner at Sullivan & Cromwell in Manhattan, discovered this firsthand when planning a solo trip to Morocco last year. "I started researching riads in Marrakech's medina on a Sunday afternoon and found myself still comparing La Mamounia versus Royal Mansour at midnight three days later," she recalls. "I billed 47 hours that week but spent another 12 on vacation planning. At $450 per hour, I essentially paid myself $5,400 to be a travel agent."

> "I started researching riads in Marrakech's medina on a Sunday afternoon and found myself still comparing La Mamounia versus Royal Mansour at midnight three days later. At $450 per hour, I essentially paid myself $5,400 to be a travel agent."

The opportunity cost becomes even more stark when you factor in the mental bandwidth required for travel planning. Those hours aren't just lost billable time—they're cognitive resources diverted from high-value work or, frankly, actual rest.

---

## Why solo travel amplifies the problem

Business travel has corporate assistants and established protocols. Group leisure travel often gets delegated to the most detail-oriented person in the friend group. Solo travel? That responsibility falls squarely on you.

Solo travelers face unique challenges that multiply planning time:

• **No shared decision-making burden** means every choice—from American Airlines versus Delta to The Ritz-Carlton versus Park Hyatt—requires individual research
• **Safety considerations** add layers of complexity, especially for female travelers heading to Istanbul's Beyoğlu district or Rio's Copacabana after dark  
• **Dining reservations** become crucial when you can't rely on group dynamics to discover restaurants
• **Activity coordination** requires advance booking since you can't split up and reconvene like couples or groups

Michael Rodriguez, a VP at Salesforce who travels solo quarterly, puts it bluntly: "When I'm traveling with my wife, we can wing dinner plans. When I'm alone in Barcelona, I need that 8pm table at Disfrutar reserved two months out, or I'm eating Marriott room service."

The stakes feel higher because there's no travel companion to help recover from poor planning decisions.

## Beyond the math: the quality factor

The financial argument alone would be compelling, but many professionals discover an unexpected benefit: better trips.

Travel concierges possess institutional knowledge that casual researchers can't match. They know that The Greenwich Hotel in Tribeca has the best solo dining scene, that Delta reliably upgrades Diamond Medallion members on the 6pm JFK-LAX flight, and that Osteria di Giovanni in Florence actually welcomes solo diners at the bar counter.

Emma Thompson, a cardiothoracic surgeon at Mount Sinai who switched to using a concierge service after a disastrous self-planned trip to Greece, explains: "I spent six hours researching Santorini hotels and still ended up at Andronis Luxury Suites, which turned out to be entirely couples-focused with shared infinity pools. My concierge would have steered me toward Mystique or Vedema instead—properties that actually cater to solo travelers."

Professional trip planners also maintain relationships with properties like The St. Regis and suppliers that can translate into tangible benefits—room upgrades, reservations at fully-booked establishments like Eleven Madison Park, or priority rebooking during travel disruptions.

## The loyalty program consideration

One concern that keeps professionals from outsourcing travel planning: protecting their carefully cultivated Marriott Bonvoy Titanium or United Premier 1K status and points balances.

The best concierge services address this directly. They book American Airlines flights through your AAdvantage account, ensure Marriott stays credit properly to your Bonvoy number, and can even factor your Hyatt Globalist status into their recommendations for properties like Park Hyatt Tokyo versus Grand Hyatt Tokyo.

This isn't about abandoning the loyalty game—it's about playing it more efficiently. A good concierge knows that your Marriott Titanium status makes The Ritz-Carlton obvious over independent hotels, and that your Delta Diamond medallion changes the calculus on routing through Atlanta versus Detroit.

## When outsourcing makes sense

Not every trip justifies professional planning assistance. Weekend getaways to familiar cities like Boston or simple point-to-point travel from New York to Los Angeles rarely require extensive research.

But certain trip profiles scream for professional help:

• **Complex multi-city itineraries** like Tokyo-Kyoto-Osaka with bullet train coordination
• **Unfamiliar international destinations** like first-time visits to Morocco's imperial cities or Vietnam's northern provinces
• **Special occasion trips** like 40th birthday celebrations in French wine country
• **Last-minute travel** where Singapore Airlines availability changes hourly
• **Trips during peak seasons** like cherry blossom season in Japan or high season in Amalfi Coast

The sweet spot seems to be professionals earning $200,000+ annually who take 3-4 leisure trips per year. For this demographic, outsourcing travel planning often pays for itself within a single complex booking to places like multi-city European itineraries or Southeast Asian adventures.

> "I realized I was treating vacation planning like a hobby when it should have been treated like any other service I outsource—my CPA handles taxes, my housekeeper handles cleaning, my mechanic handles car maintenance. My time is finite; why waste it researching whether to stay in Rome's Trastevere or Monti neighborhood when someone else knows the answer immediately?"

## The broader shift in professional services

Travel planning outsourcing reflects a broader trend among high earners: the recognition that time arbitrage applies beyond traditional business functions like legal work and accounting.

The same professionals who wouldn't dream of doing their own taxes or securities filings have been oddly resistant to outsourcing travel planning. Perhaps it's because trip research feels enjoyable in theory, even when it becomes tedious in practice—like comparing 47 different ryokans in Hakone on TripAdvisor.

But as remote work blurs the boundaries between personal and professional time, many executives are applying business efficiency principles to their personal lives. If you can delegate researching whether The Langham Sydney or Park Hyatt Sydney has better harbor views and the cost is reasonable relative to your $300 hourly rate, why wouldn't you?

## Making the transition

For professionals considering this shift, the key is finding a service that understands your specific needs and preferences. The best concierges learn your travel style quickly—whether you prefer Rosewood properties or Aman resorts, aisle or window seats, and whether you're comfortable walking 15 minutes from Covent Garden hotels to West End theaters.

Start with a single complex trip to test the service. A two-week solo journey through Vietnam and Cambodia or a last-minute long weekend in Copenhagen provides plenty of opportunities to evaluate their expertise and attention to detail.

Most importantly, be specific about your preferences and constraints. The more information you provide upfront—Marriott Bonvoy Titanium status, gluten-free dietary needs, preference for hotels under 100 rooms, $2,000 per night budget ceiling—the better your results will be.

The math is straightforward: when your time is genuinely valuable, spending it on tasks others can do more efficiently makes zero sense. Solo travel planning has simply become the latest casualty of this economic reality.
