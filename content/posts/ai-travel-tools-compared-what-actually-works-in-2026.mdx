After testing fifteen AI travel tools this year, here's what I learned: most are sophisticated recommendation engines masquerading as booking platforms. They'll tell you to book the Conrad Tokyo for £485/night, suggest taking the 14:20 JAL flight from Heathrow, even recommend visiting during the shoulder season in October—but when it comes to actually purchasing that £2,847 business class ticket to Tokyo, you're suddenly redirected to three different websites.

The gap between recommendation and execution has never been wider. While AI has revolutionized how we discover travel options, the booking process remains frustratingly fragmented across multiple platforms, each with its own fees, policies, and booking quirks.

---

## The recommendation giants: Smart but incomplete

ChatGPT and Claude dominate the travel planning conversation, and for good reason. Ask either about a three-week Southeast Asia itinerary with a £3,500 budget, and you'll receive thoughtful, well-researched suggestions that would have taken a human agent hours to compile.

ChatGPT excels at creating detailed day-by-day itineraries with specific recommendations like staying at the Mad Monkey Hostel in Siem Reap for £18/night or dining at Gaggan Anand in Bangkok for their progressive Indian tasting menu at £165 per person. During testing, it accurately suggested avoiding Kyoto's Maruyama Park during cherry blossom season for budget travelers, recommending Yoshino's Mount Daikaku with 30,000 cherry trees and 90% fewer crowds.

Claude takes a more analytical approach, weighing practical considerations like visa requirements, seasonal weather patterns, and transportation logistics. It correctly identified that flying into Ho Chi Minh City's Tan Son Nhat Airport versus Hanoi's Noi Bai could save £340 on a London-Vietnam route when continuing overland to Cambodia, factoring in the more efficient southern border crossings.

But here's where both hit a wall: they can't see real inventory at the Park Hyatt Saigon, check actual prices on Vietnam Airlines, or make bookings. That perfect itinerary comes with a disclaimer to "check current prices and availability."

> "The most detailed recommendation becomes worthless if you can't actually book what's being suggested at the price quoted."

Google's Bard (now Gemini) attempts to bridge this gap by surfacing real flight prices from British Airways at £847 and hotel rates at the InterContinental Hanoi Westlake for £156/night. It's genuinely useful for quick price comparisons and can identify deals—like spotting an £89 easyJet London-Edinburgh flight when British Airways hovers around £180.

However, clicking "book" redirects you to Booking.com or Expedia where that £156 rate jumps to £189 including taxes, availability disappears for your specific dates, or free cancellation policies differ from what was initially displayed.

## The booking specialists: Half-solved problems

Expedia's AI chat assistant represents the next evolution: a tool that can both recommend and book. In practice, it works well for straightforward requests. Ask for a weekend in Barcelona under £600 total, and it surfaces actual bookable packages like three nights at Hotel Barcelona Gothic Quarter (£78/night) plus Ryanair flights from London Stansted (£67 return).

The limitation? Complexity defeats it. Multi-city trips, specific airline preferences, or loyalty program requirements often result in generic suggestions that ignore your stated preferences. During testing, requesting "Star Alliance flights to Bangkok to maximize United Premier status miles" returned random Qatar Airways and Emirates options across rival alliances.

Kayak's AI features focus on price prediction and deal alerts. Its "price forecast" correctly predicted a £200 drop in London-New York fares from £598 to £398 three weeks ahead of travel on Virgin Atlantic, saving substantial money for flexible travelers. But again, it stops at recommendations rather than completing bookings.

Skyscanner's recent AI integration offers intelligent date suggestions and alternative routing options. It successfully identified that flying London-Bangkok via Turkish Airlines through Istanbul Atatürk cost £340 less than direct Thai Airways options (£687 vs £1,027), with only two additional hours of travel time. Smart routing, but you're still redirected to airline websites for actual booking where dynamic pricing often increases costs.

## The execution gap: Where AI still struggles

The fundamental problem isn't intelligence—it's integration. Current AI travel tools excel at processing vast amounts of data and generating personalized recommendations for properties like the Shangri-La Bangkok or flights on Singapore Airlines. They stumble when interfacing with the complex web of airline booking systems, hotel inventory platforms like SynXis or HotelBeds, and payment processing requirements.

Real-time inventory remains the biggest challenge. AI tools often display the Mandarin Oriental Bangkok at £425/night when actual availability is £510/night for your specific dates. In volatile markets, this creates a frustrating cycle of recommendations that can't be fulfilled at the quoted price.

Loyalty programs present another complexity layer. While AI can recommend Emirates flight EK29 departing at 08:30 as the "optimal" choice, it can't account for your Skywards Gold status enabling lounge access, complimentary upgrades to premium economy, or the fact that booking through Emirates directly earns 125% miles versus 50% through third-party sites.

> "The future of travel AI isn't about smarter recommendations—it's about seamless execution from initial search through final confirmation email."

Payment processing adds yet another friction point. Booking the Four Seasons George V Paris through Hotels.com has different cancellation policies than booking directly with Four Seasons, potentially costing £680 in change fees that AI recommendations rarely highlight upfront.

## What actually works: The verdict

For trip planning inspiration and detailed itineraries spanning places like the temples of Bagan or markets of Marrakech's Djemaa el-Fnaa, ChatGPT and Claude remain unmatched. Their contextual understanding and creative suggestions surpass any traditional booking site's generic recommendations.

For straightforward bookings of standard trips—weekend city breaks, simple round-trip flights on major carriers like British Airways or Lufthansa—Expedia's AI booking works adequately. The interface is familiar and the process relatively smooth for simple requests.
