## The death of travel apps nobody asked for

Every airline, hotel chain, and booking platform wants you to download their app. Delta insists theirs is essential for SkyMiles members. Marriott claims exclusive app-only rates at properties like the Edition Times Square. Booking.com sends push notifications at 2 AM about "deals ending soon" for hotels in cities you've never searched.

The result? The average American has 80 apps on their phone but actively uses only 9. Travel apps get downloaded before trips and forgotten after, cluttering digital lives without adding real value.

> "We're drowning in apps that solve problems we didn't know we had while ignoring the ones we actually face every day - like finding a decent hotel in downtown Austin that isn't $400/night during SXSW."

Text messaging, however, has 98% open rates compared to 20% for emails and 3% for push notifications. When someone texts you about your Paris flight being delayed, you see it. When the Air France app notification tries the same thing, it joins the digital noise.

## Why your brain prefers conversations over interfaces

Booking travel through traditional websites forces you to think like a database. Departure city: dropdown menu showing LAX, Burbank, Long Beach, and Ontario. Dates: calendar widget that doesn't show price variations. Class of service: radio buttons between "Economy" and "Premium Economy" with no explanation of the $200 difference.

It's digital paint-by-numbers when what you really want to say is: "I need to get to Tokyo for that conference, leaving Friday afternoon, something comfortable but not crazy expensive - maybe premium economy on ANA or JAL if the price is reasonable."

Human language is nuanced, contextual, efficient. When you text a friend about dinner plans, you don't fill out forms. You say "Thai place at 8?" and they understand your preferences, your usual spots like Night + Market in West Hollywood, your typical timing.

Text-first booking works the same way. Describe your trip naturally—"Weekend in Nashville, nice hotel downtown near Broadway, flights from LAX under $400"—and let intelligence, whether human or artificial, translate that into actionable options.

> "The best interface is often no interface at all, just natural conversation that gets things done without making you think like a computer."

Text-first services bridge this gap perfectly.
